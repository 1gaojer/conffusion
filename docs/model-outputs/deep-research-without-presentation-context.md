## Why this is a natural generative modeling problem

**Observed and known.** Proteins function through **ensembles**, not just single structures, and there is explicit recognition in the AlphaFold3 commentary literature that moving from static structure prediction to molecular behavior requires modeling **dynamic conformational ensembles**. Antibodies are an especially plausible testbed because their variable regions are structurally constrained overall but locally flexible, and CDR-H3 is unusually diverse relative to other loops. Recent structural analyses also suggest that, upon binding, many CDRs change only modestly on average, whereas CDR-H3 is more likely to carry substantial conformational uncertainty. ţłÇciteţłéturn0search14ţłéturn4search17ţłéturn4search0ţłéturn4search15ţłü

That makes your problem look mathematically like learning a **conditional distribution**
\[
p(\text{conformer} \mid \text{antibody sequence}, \text{possibly antigen/context}, \text{teacher pipeline state})
\]
or, more practically,
\[
p(\text{conformer cluster} \mid \text{target})
\]
plus a local decoder for structural variation. This is a very natural fit for **diffusion**, **flow matching**, or **transformer-based latent models**, because all three are built to represent multimodal distributions rather than single point estimates. In proteins, these ideas are already established: RFdiffusion and FrameDiff showed that diffusion on 3D protein representations is tractable; AlphaFlow and ESMFlow adapted single-structure predictors into ensemble samplers; Str2Str showed zero-shot conformation sampling from structural priors; and recent structure-language models used discrete latent structure tokens to generate diverse conformations more efficiently. ţłÇciteţłéturn10view4ţłéturn12view6ţłéturn12view0ţłéturn8search4ţłéturn12view5ţłü

**Plausible interpretation.** Antibody conformers are, in some sense, an *easier* generative target than arbitrary full-protein design because most of the framework is not where the entropy lives. The generative burden is concentrated in a smaller subspace: VH/VL orientation, local loop geometry, especially H3, and interface-adjacent side-chain packing. That structure suggests that a thesis centered on **localized generation or cluster-level generation** is substantially more realistic than one centered on unrestricted full all-atom synthesis from noise. The current antibody structure literature also reinforces this asymmetry: IgFold, ABlooper, and related tools show that antibody structure prediction is already highly tractable in the framework regions, while H3 remains the principal challenge. ţłÇciteţłéturn10view9ţłéturn5search3ţłéturn4search17ţłü

**Speculation, but informed.** If GaeunÔÇÖs teacher ensembles are genuinely rich and not just repetitive perturbations around a few modes, then a learned generator could plausibly recover their **support**ÔÇöthe major conformer familiesÔÇöeven if it cannot recover their exact physical probabilities. For downstream representation learning or retrieval, support coverage may matter more than exact Boltzmann weighting. That is exactly why I think this can work as a **scientific-computing distillation problem** even if it would be too strong to market as a true physics problem. AlphaFlow, BioEmu, and EBA all point in the same direction: the field increasingly accepts learned ensemble samplers as useful surrogates, but the strongest papers are careful about what distribution they are actually learning and how they validate it. ţłÇciteţłéturn12view0ţłéturn14search4ţłéturn15search2ţłü

## How this differs from image diffusion and which model families actually matter

The analogy to image or video diffusion is **useful but limited**. RFdiffusion itself explicitly drew inspiration from image DDPMs, but proteins are not images with a different tokenizer. Protein generation must respect **SE(3) geometry**, chain continuity, bond and torsion constraints, sterics, chirality, and often equivariance. A visually plausible image can be biologically meaningless; a 1ÔÇô2 ├ů local structural mistake in a paratope can destroy a binding interface. Protein models therefore use very different representations: residue frames, torsions, equivariant graphs, pairwise geometric features, or discrete structure tokens rather than unconstrained pixel grids. ţłÇciteţłéturn10view4ţłéturn12view6ţłéturn0search0ţłü

The biggest difference from your hobby experience with Stable Diffusion and LoRA is that **ÔÇťstyle adaptationÔÇŁ is not the right abstraction here**. In image LoRA, you often adapt a gigantic pretrained generative prior to a narrow visual concept. The closest biological analogue would be **parameter-efficient adaptation on top of a strong pretrained protein or antibody prior**, not training a structure generator from scratch on a few hundred targets. That idea is already validated indirectly by AbMAP, S┬▓ALM, IgBert/IgT5, IgFold, and ESM3: strong pretrained sequence or sequence-structure priors are increasingly the backbone for antibody modeling. If you do anything ÔÇťLoRA-like,ÔÇŁ it should be on top of a large pretrained protein or antibody model, not as a standalone small-data generative model. ţłÇciteţłéturn20search4ţłéturn12view4ţłéturn23view0ţłéturn10view9ţłéturn2search3ţłü

The model families that matter separate into three groups.

**General protein structure generators.** RFdiffusion and FrameDiff are important because they establish the geometric machinery, but they are aimed mainly at *designable backbone generation*, not at target-specific equilibrium-like ensembles for existing antibodies. They are conceptually relevant, but not the nearest baseline for your practical problem. ţłÇciteţłéturn10view4ţłéturn12view6ţłü

**Protein ensemble generators.** AlphaFlow and ESMFlow are directly relevant because they recast single-structure predictors as **sequence-conditioned ensemble samplers** and show better precision-diversity tradeoffs than MSA subsampling. AlphaFlow-Lit pushes the same family toward efficiency by fine-tuning only the lighter-weight structure module and reporting roughly **47├Ś** faster sampling while staying on par with AlphaFlow. BioEmu is even more important strategically: it shows that large-scale ensemble emulation can generate **thousands of statistically independent structures per hour on a single GPU**, integrating MD, static structures, and experimental stability signals. BBFlow, aSAM, Str2Str, and structure-language models then show alternative recipes: geometry-only flows, latent diffusion on MD data, zero-shot conformation translation, and discrete token models with large speedups. For thesis framing, these papers collectively say: **ensemble modeling is real, but the winning architectural choice depends on what supervision you have.** ţłÇciteţłéturn12view0ţłéturn24view0ţłéturn14search4ţłéturn6search5ţłéturn13search2ţłéturn8search4ţłéturn12view5ţłü

**Antibody-specific generators.** DiffAb was one of the first diffusion models for antigen-conditioned antibody CDR sequence-structure design. AbDiffuser pushed toward full-atom antibody generation with physics-informed priors and reported in vitro hits. IgDiff adapted SE(3) diffusion to antibody variable domains and experimentally validated designed antibodies. dyMEAN, DiffForce, AntibodyFlow, dyAb, and AbFlow are all relevant as neighboring ideas, but most of them are primarily **design** papers, not **ensemble compression** papers. That is both good and bad news: good because there is room for novelty; bad because you should not overstate precedent. There is still a real gap between ÔÇťgenerate good antibodiesÔÇŁ and ÔÇťcompress a large target-specific antibody conformer ensemble without losing downstream utility.ÔÇŁ ţłÇciteţłéturn11search12ţłéturn12view3ţłéturn10view2ţłéturn1search18ţłéturn15search0ţłéturn4academia28ţłéturn4academia27ţłéturn20academia30ţłü

## Data reality and what your dataset can actually support

**Observed and known.** Public antibody structural data are much smaller than general sequence corpora. As of the current SAbDab statistics page, SAbDab contains **10,840 antibody structures**, **8,633** with at least one paired VH/VL, and **10,200** with antigen; in contrast, OAS contains **over one billion sequences** from **over 80 studies**, and PLAbDab contains **over 150,000 paired antibody sequences** with **over 65,000 unique** entries and linked 3D structural models. That asymmetry matters: sequence priors are abundant, but *ensemble-quality structural priors are scarce*. ţłÇciteţłéturn17search6ţłéturn23view2ţłéturn3search15ţłü

That means your ÔÇťfew hundred to few thousand antibody conformer ensemblesÔÇŁ should be thought of in two different ways. If you are asking whether that is enough to train a **foundation model from scratch**, the answer is probably **no**. Existing successful antibody or protein models usually lean on either huge sequence corpora, broad structure corpora, MD-scale data, or strong pretrained folding models. If you are asking whether it is enough to train a **teacher-distillation model**, **cluster predictor**, or **local perturbation generator** on top of strong priors, the answer becomes **yes, plausibly**. Your raw count of millions of conformers is helpful for learning local density and ranking functions, but the **effective sample size** is much closer to the number of distinct antibodies and antigen contexts than to the number of PDB files, because all conformers from one target are heavily correlated. ţłÇciteţłéturn12view0ţłéturn14search4ţłéturn6search5ţłéturn20search4ţłéturn12view4ţłü

This is where leakage becomes existential. If train and test contain conformers from the same antibody, same clonotype, near-identical CDR-H3s, or even adjacent points in the same teacher-generated ensemble, your model can appear miraculous while doing almost nothing useful. Leakage-aware train/test construction is now a recognized methodological issue across biological ML, including interaction benchmarks and antibody benchmarks; AbBiBench explicitly curates splits to avoid mutant-complex leakage, and emerging retrieval work such as CALM emphasizes **leakage-controlled SAbDab splits**. For your dataset, I would insist on splits at the **target ensemble level**, plus sequence-identity or clonotype controls, plus antigen-family controls if downstream antigen retrieval is part of the story. ţłÇciteţłéturn16search0ţłéturn16search7ţłéturn22view1ţłéturn21search0ţłü

What I would want before believing the data are thesis-worthy is very concrete. I would want to know: how many **unique heavy/light pairs** there are; how many **independent antigens or antigen families**; whether the teacher pipeline begins from one seed or several; whether ensemble modes are mostly **H3-local** or include larger VH/VL motions; what the final filters are; and whether the retained conformers represent many genuinely distinct clusters or just many near-duplicates. Without those answers, ÔÇť1.5 million conformersÔÇŁ could mean ÔÇťa gold mineÔÇŁ or ÔÇťthe same few geometries repeated at industrial scale.ÔÇŁ That is not a rhetorical flourish; it is the central data question. ţłÇciteţłéturn4search15ţłéturn17search6ţłéturn22view1ţłü

## The formulation I would actually recommend

If your real question is **how much can the ProtHunt/AF3 ensemble be shrunk while preserving variability and downstream quality**, then the strongest formulation is **not full generative modeling first**. It is **ensemble compression first, generation second**.

My recommended order is this:

- **Best minimum-viable formulation:** learn a **rank-and-select compressor**. Start from the full teacher ensemble and learn to keep a much smaller subset that preserves geometric diversity, cluster coverage, embedding diversity, and downstream antigen-specific retrieval or prediction. This directly answers GaeunÔÇÖs optimization question and gives you an honest baseline against random subsampling and hand-crafted clustering. It is also exactly the sort of contribution that can still be a good thesis if it yields, say, a **10ÔÇô50├Ś** reduction with no downstream loss. The existence of stronger function-aware evaluation frameworks like AbBiBench is a good reminder that compressing the right signals matters more than generating pretty structures. ţłÇciteţłéturn22view1ţłéturn20academia27ţłü

- **Best thesis-worthy generative extension:** learn a **latent conformer-cluster model**. Concretely: cluster each teacher ensemble; encode conformers into a target-conditioned latent space; learn the distribution over cluster identities or continuous latent centers; then decode a small number of representative conformers. This is where structure-language modeling ideas become attractive, because they naturally separate **mode discovery** from **local reconstruction** and are much more efficient than full all-atom diffusion. ESM3 and SLM/ESMDiff are relevant here, not because you will copy them directly, but because they show that discrete structure-token abstractions can be competitive and fast. ţłÇciteţłéturn2search3ţłéturn12view5ţłü

- **Most realistic continuous generator:** a **local CDR/paratope flow or diffusion model** conditioned on sequence, framework coordinates, and perhaps a single seed structure. I would strongly prefer **residue frames or torsions** over Cartesian all-atom generation for this. FrameDiff shows the geometry is principled; AlphaFlow and BBFlow show flow-based ensemble generation can be efficient; antibody structure predictors such as IgFold and ABlooper suggest that most of the framework can be treated as solved conditioning context while the model focuses on difficult variable loops. ţłÇciteţłéturn12view6ţłéturn12view0ţłéturn6search5ţłéturn10view9ţłéturn5search3ţłü

- **Too ambitious for a normal thesis:** full **all-atom whole-antibody generation from noise** with claims of improved physical realism over the teacher. AbDiffuser and IgDiff show that de novo antibody generation is possible, but these are broad design models backed by stronger priors and broader data than you are describing. For your problem, spending most of your capacity regenerating easy framework atoms is a poor use of modeling budget. ţłÇciteţłéturn12view3ţłéturn10view2ţłü

The deeper conceptual fork is whether you want to model a **biophysical distribution** or a **teacher distribution**. I would frame the thesis explicitly as **distillation of an expensive computational pipeline** unless you have independent physical validation. If you train on AF3/ProtHunt outputs, then by default you are learning the support and biases of that pipeline. That is perfectly acceptable if the downstream objective is retrieval, representation learning, or predictive utility. It is **not** acceptable to imply that you have learned the true solution ensemble. The papers that make the strongest equilibrium claimsÔÇöBioEmu, AlphaFlow-on-MD, EBA, aSAMÔÇöearn those claims either through MD supervision, thermodynamic targets, or explicit physical alignment. ţłÇciteţłéturn12view1ţłéturn14search4ţłéturn15search2ţłéturn13search2ţłü

## Baselines, evaluation, and the ways this project can fail

The most important skeptical point is this: **a diffusion model is not the baseline to beat.** The baseline to beat is **smart subsampling of the teacher**, because your actual problem is cost reduction. I would require, at minimum, comparison against random subsampling; stratified subsampling by teacher score; k-medoids or farthest-point sampling in structural space; and an ÔÇťAF3-liteÔÇŁ condition where the original pipeline is rerun with fewer seeds or fewer recycles. Recent benchmarks are increasingly explicit that distributional quality or nativeness alone is not enough; downstream functional relevance has to be checked. ţłÇciteţłéturn22view1ţłéturn20academia27ţłü

Your evaluation stack should have four layers. First, **geometry and validity**: bond and angle sanity, steric clash rates, chain continuity, Ramachandran or torsion plausibility, and loop closure. Second, **ensemble fidelity** to the teacher: cluster coverage, pairwise RMSD distributions, CDR-H3 torsion or backbone statistics, per-residue positional variance, and ensemble precision-diversity style metrics similar in spirit to AlphaFlow and BioEmu. Third, **representation-level diversity**: does the compressed or generated set preserve the spread of embeddings from the downstream antibody representation model, whether that is AntiBERTy-family, AbMAP-family, S┬▓ALM-family, or a custom structure encoder? Fourth, and most important, **task performance**: antigen retrieval, disease-panel prediction, or whatever downstream endpoint Gaeun actually cares about. If the downstream metric does not hold, nothing else matters. ţłÇciteţłéturn12view0ţłéturn14search4ţłéturn20search4ţłéturn12view4ţłü

There is a very recent and useful warning here. AbBiBench reports that, across affinity maturation and design settings, **structure-conditioned inverse folding models** outperform several other classes, including diffusion-based models, on both affinity correlation and generation tasks. That does **not** mean diffusion is bad. It means the right question is not ÔÇťcan diffusion generate?ÔÇŁ but ÔÇťdoes my model preserve or improve the downstream signal I care about?ÔÇŁ A thesis that proves a compressed selector or inverse-folding-aware reranker is better than a fancy generator is still a good thesis. In fact, it is often the stronger one. ţłÇciteţłéturn20academia27ţłéturn22view1ţłü

The main failure modes are not subtle. The model may simply learn **teacher artifacts**. It may overfit because of **family leakage** through SAbDab-like sources, clonotypes, or near-duplicate conformers. It may preserve geometric diversity but lose the only modes that matter for retrieval. It may generate structures that look plausible but are not realizable or not useful. And if there is no independent experimental or physics-based validation, it may tempt you into **overclaiming antigen specificity or dynamics**. These are not hypothetical pathologies; the broader ML-biology literature now treats leakage and overoptimistic generalization as systemic risks. ţłÇciteţłéturn16search0ţłéturn16search7ţłéturn22view1ţłéturn21search0ţłü

My threshold for success would therefore be plain and unsentimental. If a learned method can reduce a 1,500ÔÇô2,000-conformer teacher ensemble to, say, **50ÔÇô200 conformers** while preserving most of the teacherÔÇÖs cluster coverage and essentially all downstream retrieval or prediction performanceÔÇöand ideally saving an order of magnitude of computeÔÇöthat is a strong and publishable result. If a synthetic generator cannot beat a strong clustering or selection baseline, then the project should pivot and say so. That pivot would not be a failure; it would be the scientifically honest outcome. ţłÇciteţłéturn24view0ţłéturn12view5ţłéturn14search4ţłü

## A feasible thesis framing, a research plan, and the questions I would ask before committing

The title I would personally endorse would sound something like: **ÔÇťCompute-efficient distillation of antibody conformer ensembles for structure-aware representation learning.ÔÇŁ** That is compelling because it is specific, useful, and not inflated. It says you are solving a real bottleneck in antibody ML. It does **not** pretend that you have solved antibody dynamics. If you want a slightly more ambitious variant, make it: **ÔÇťTarget-conditioned compression and localized generation of antibody conformer ensembles for downstream specificity modeling.ÔÇŁ** I would avoid words like *equilibrium*, *Boltzmann*, or *true dynamics* unless you have independent physical validation. ţłÇciteţłéturn12view0ţłéturn14search4ţłéturn22view1ţłü

For the **first three months**, I would do almost no fancy modeling. I would audit the dataset, build leakage-safe splits, quantify redundancy, cluster each ensemble, and establish brutal baselines: random k-subsampling, k-medoids in structural space, H3-focused clustering, and reduced-seed AF3. I would also freeze one or two downstream representation models and define the primary endpoint early. If a simple clustering baseline already gives a 20├Ś compression at negligible downstream loss, that is important information and should shape the thesis. ţłÇciteţłéturn16search0ţłéturn22view1ţłéturn21search0ţłü

For the **first six months**, I would train a learned **selector/compressor** and at most one lightweight **latent model**. The selector should use sequence, framework structure, loop features, and teacher metadata to predict which conformers matter. The latent model should operate over cluster centroids or residue-frame perturbations, not raw all-atom coordinates. Around this stage, I would decide whether generation is really adding value or whether representation-preserving selection is the more defensible endpoint. Methods like AlphaFlow-Lit, BBFlow, and structure-language models are good inspiration here because they explicitly trade some architectural grandeur for speed and tractability. ţłÇciteţłéturn24view0ţłéturn6search5ţłéturn12view5ţłü

For the **full twelve-month arc**, I would only add a more ambitious generator if the earlier phases justify it. The natural phase-two extension is a **local flow or diffusion decoder** over CDR-centered variables, optionally with physical guidance or post hoc reranking. If that works, great. If not, the thesis can still be excellent if it shows that a carefully learned compression policy achieves large compute savings and preserves downstream performance better than naive generation. That, to me, is a much stronger thesis than a flashy but weakly validated generative story. ţłÇciteţłéturn15search0ţłéturn15search2ţłéturn20academia27ţłü

Before committing, I would ask **Gaeun** and **Sophia** the following very directly:

- What is the exact count of **unique antibodies**, not conformers, after deduplication by paired VH/VL sequence and clonotype?
- What fraction of ensemble diversity is in **CDR-H3** versus broader VH/VL or framework motion?
- What are the current **teacher filters** and scores, and are those scores themselves good predictors of downstream utility?
